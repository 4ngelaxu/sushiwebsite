Ingredients

All sushi has a base of specially prepared rice, complemented with other ingredients. Traditional Japanese sushi consists of rice flavored with vinegar sauce and various raw or cooked ingredients.Sushi (すし, 寿司, 鮨, 鮓, is a Japanese dish of prepared vinegared rice, usually with some sugar and salt, plus a variety of ingredients), such as vegetables, and any meat, but most commonly seafood (often raw but can be cooked. It is often served with pickled ginger (gari), wasabi, and soy sauce.

Preparation

Nori

The dark green seaweed wrappers used in makimono are called nori (海苔). Nori is a type of algae traditionally cultivated in the harbors of Japan. Originally, algae was scraped from dock pilings, rolled out into thin, edible sheets, and dried in the sun, similar to making rice paper.

Sushi-meshi

Sushi-meshi (鮨飯) (also known as su-meshi (酢飯), shari (舎利), or gohan (ご飯)) is a preparation of white, short-grained, Japanese rice mixed with a dressing consisting of rice vinegar, sugar, salt, and occasionally kombu and sake. It must be cooled to room temperature before being used for a sushi filling, or it will get too sticky while seasoned.

Gu

The ingredients used inside sushi are called gu and are, typically, varieties of fish. Commonly used fish are tuna (maguro, shiro-maguro), Japanese amberjack, yellowtail (hamachi), snapper (kurodai), mackerel (saba), and salmon (sake)

Vegetables

Variety of plant based products are often included too; such as avocado, cucumber, pickled radish (takuan), pickled vegetables (tsukemono), fermented soybeans (nattō), asparagus, yam and sweet corn (sometimes mixed with mayonnaise).

Makisu

Makisu (巻き簾) is a bamboo rolling mat In Japanese cooking woven from bamboo and cotton string and is most commonly used to roll up sushi.

Presentation

Traditionally, sushi is cut up into bite sized pieces constituting a single roll order, and is served on minimalist Japanese-style, geometric, mono- or duo-tone wood or lacquer plates, keeping with this cuisine's aesthetic qualities.

Serving

Sushi is also commonly eaten with condiments. Sushi may be dipped in shōyu (soy sauce), and is usually flavored with wasabi, a piquant paste made from the grated stem of the Wasabia japonica plant. Japanese-style mayonnaise is a common condiment in Japan on salmon, pork, and other sushi cuts.
